\section{Concurrency}

\begin{frame}{What is Concurrency?}
  \begin{block}{Definition}
    \textbf{Concurrency} in the context of databases refers to the ability of multiple users or
    processes to access and modify the same database data \textbf{simultaneously}, without
    compromising data integrity or consistency.
  \end{block}

  \medskip
  In any real production system -- an online shop, a banking application, an ERP -- hundreds or
  thousands of transactions may be executing at the exact same moment. This is not an edge case;
  it is the normal operating mode.

  \medskip
  Without proper concurrency control, the following problems arise:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Users overwrite each other's changes (\emph{lost update}).
    \item A user reads data that is about to be rolled back (\emph{dirty read}).
    \item A user reads the same data twice and gets different results (\emph{non-repeatable read}).
    \item A query sees a different set of rows on two successive executions (\emph{phantom read}).
  \end{itemize}

  \medskip
  \textbf{Concurrency control} is one of the core responsibilities of any DBMS. The goal is to
  allow maximum parallelism while guaranteeing that the result is always equivalent to some
  \emph{serial} (one-at-a-time) execution of those same transactions -- a property called
  \textbf{serializability}.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Problem 1: Lost Update}
  \textbf{Scenario:} two users simultaneously update the unit price of the same product.

  \medskip
  \begin{tabular}{@{}p{3.8cm}p{3.8cm}p{3.5cm}@{}}
    \toprule
    \textbf{User A} & \textbf{User B} & \textbf{What happens?} \\
    \midrule
    Read price (= 10.00) & & \\
    & Read price (= 10.00) & Both see old value \\
    Compute 10.00 + 2.00 & & \\
    & Compute 10.00 $-$ 1.00 & \\
    Write price = 12.00 & & A commits first \\
    COMMIT & & \\
    & Write price = 9.00 & B overwrites A! \\
    & COMMIT & A's change is LOST \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}

  \medskip
  \begin{alertblock}{Result}
    Both users read the same original value (10.00), calculated their own updates independently,
    and the \emph{last writer wins}. User A's increase to 12.00 is completely overwritten and
    lost. The final price is 9.00 instead of the logically correct 11.00 (or 8.00, depending on
    which update was intended to be the ``final'' one).
  \end{alertblock}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Problem 2: Dirty Read (Uncommitted Dependency)}
  \textbf{Scenario:} an ATM reads an account balance that is subsequently rolled back.

  \medskip
  \begin{tabular}{@{}p{3.5cm}p{4cm}p{3.2cm}@{}}
    \toprule
    \textbf{ATM} & \textbf{Bank Server} & \textbf{What happens?} \\
    \midrule
    & BEGIN: incoming transfer of \EUR{500} & \\
    & Balance: 300 + 500 = 800 (in cache) & \\
    Read balance = 800 & & Reads \emph{dirty} data \\
    COMMIT (displays \EUR{800}) & & \\
    & ROLLBACK (transfer failed) & Balance reverts to 300 \\
    \multicolumn{3}{l}{\textit{The ATM showed \EUR{800} but the real balance is \EUR{300}.}} \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}

  \medskip
  \begin{alertblock}{Result}
    The ATM read data from a transaction that had not yet committed -- ``dirty'' (uncommitted)
    data. When the bank server rolls back, the ATM has already confirmed a balance that no longer
    exists. This can lead the customer to withdraw money they do not actually have.
  \end{alertblock}

  \medskip
  A \textbf{dirty read} is prevented at isolation level \texttt{READ COMMITTED} or higher.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Problem 3: Inconsistent Analysis (Non-Repeatable / Phantom Read)}
  \textbf{Scenario:} an auditor totals three account balances while a transfer runs concurrently.\\
  Initial state: Account\,1 = \EUR{400}, Account\,2 = \EUR{300}, Account\,3 = \EUR{700}
  (total = \EUR{1\,400}).

  \medskip
  \begin{tabular}{@{}p{3.5cm}p{4cm}p{3cm}@{}}
    \toprule
    \textbf{User A (audit)} & \textbf{User B (transfer)} & \textbf{Event} \\
    \midrule
    Read Acc\,1 = 400 & & \\
    Read Acc\,2 = 300 & & \\
    & Acc\,3: 700 $-$ 600 = 100 & Transfer begins \\
    & Acc\,1: 400 + 600 = 1\,000 & \\
    & COMMIT & \\
    Read Acc\,3 = 100 & & Sees new value! \\
    Sum = 400+300+100 = \textbf{800} & & \textbf{Wrong! Should be 1\,400} \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}

  \medskip
  \begin{alertblock}{Result}
    User A read the three accounts at \emph{different points in time} and saw a snapshot that
    never actually existed. \EUR{600} appears to have vanished. This is the
    \textbf{inconsistent analysis} problem -- also called a \emph{phantom read}. It is prevented
    at isolation level \texttt{REPEATABLE READ} or \texttt{SERIALIZABLE}.
  \end{alertblock}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Concurrency Control Strategies}
  Two fundamentally different philosophies for dealing with concurrent access:

  \medskip
  \begin{block}{Optimistic Concurrency Control (OCC)}
    \textbf{Assumption:} conflicts are rare. Let all transactions proceed freely.
    \begin{itemize}
      \item Transactions read and write without acquiring any locks.
      \item At \texttt{COMMIT} time, the DBMS checks whether a conflict occurred.
      \item If a conflict is detected, the transaction is \textbf{aborted and retried}.
      \item Pros: simple to implement; high throughput when conflicts are actually rare.
      \item Cons: wasted work on conflict; poor performance under high contention.
    \end{itemize}
  \end{block}

  \begin{block}{Pessimistic Concurrency Control (Lock-Based)}
    \textbf{Assumption:} conflicts are likely. Prevent them before they happen.
    \begin{itemize}
      \item A transaction must \textbf{acquire a lock} on a data item before accessing it.
      \item Other transactions that need the same item must \textbf{wait} for the lock to be
            released.
      \item Locks are released at transaction end (commit or rollback).
      \item Pros: conflicts never occur in the first place; correct even under high contention.
      \item Cons: complex; reduces parallelism; can cause \textbf{deadlocks}.
    \end{itemize}
  \end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Lock Granularity}
  A \textbf{lock} can be placed at different levels of the data hierarchy. The \emph{granularity}
  of a lock determines how much data is protected -- and how much concurrency is sacrificed.

  \medskip
  \begin{tabular}{@{}p{2cm}p{4.5cm}p{4cm}@{}}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Level} & \textbf{Description} & \textbf{Typical Use Case} \\
    \midrule
    Database & Locks the entire database; only one user can access it at a time &
               System maintenance, bulk import, schema migrations \\[4pt]
    Table    & Locks a single table; all rows are blocked for other writers &
               Few-user systems; batch operations on one table \\[4pt]
    Row (Tuple) & Locks a single row; other rows in the same table are freely accessible &
               Standard production OLTP systems (MySQL InnoDB, PostgreSQL) \\[4pt]
    Cell (Field) & Locks a single attribute value within a row &
               Extremely rare; very complex to implement \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}

  \medskip
  \begin{block}{Design principle}
    Always lock \textbf{the smallest amount of data necessary} to maintain correctness. Coarser
    locks are easier to implement but destroy concurrency. Fine-grained row-level locks maximise
    parallelism but require more overhead to track.
  \end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Shared and Exclusive Locks}
  \begin{block}{Exclusive Lock (X-Lock) -- Write Lock}
    Acquired by \texttt{UPDATE}, \texttt{INSERT}, and \texttt{DELETE} operations. While an X-lock
    is held on a data item, \textbf{no other transaction may acquire any lock} (neither shared nor
    exclusive) on that item. Writes require exclusive access.
  \end{block}

  \begin{block}{Shared Lock (S-Lock) -- Read Lock}
    Acquired by \texttt{SELECT} operations. Multiple transactions may hold S-locks on the same
    item simultaneously -- concurrent reads are always safe. However, \textbf{an X-lock cannot be
    granted} while any S-lock is held, because a write while others are reading would produce
    inconsistent results.
  \end{block}

  \medskip
  \textbf{Lock compatibility matrix:}

  \begin{tabular}{@{}lcc@{}}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Requested} $\backslash$ \textbf{Held} & \textbf{S-Lock held} & \textbf{X-Lock held} \\
    \midrule
    Request S-Lock & \textcolor{green!60!black}{Granted} & \textcolor{red}{Wait} \\
    Request X-Lock & \textcolor{red}{Wait} & \textcolor{red}{Wait} \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}

  \medskip
  \begin{examples}{Summary}
    Reads can run in parallel (S+S is fine). A write blocks all other accesses (X blocks
    everything). This implements the intuitive rule: many readers are fine; a writer needs
    exclusive access.
  \end{examples}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Deadlocks}
  \begin{alertblock}{Definition}
    A \textbf{deadlock} occurs when two or more transactions are each waiting for the other to
    release a lock -- creating a circular dependency from which no transaction can escape on its own.
  \end{alertblock}

  \medskip
  \textbf{Classic two-transaction deadlock:}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Transaction A holds an X-lock on Row 1, now requests a lock on Row 2.
    \item Transaction B holds an X-lock on Row 2, now requests a lock on Row 1.
    \item A is waiting for B; B is waiting for A. Neither can proceed. Ever.
  \end{itemize}

  \medskip
  \textbf{Detection and resolution -- two main approaches:}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Deadlock detection}: the DBMS maintains a ``waits-for'' graph (nodes are
          transactions, edges are ``is waiting for''). Periodically check for \emph{cycles} in this
          graph. When a cycle is found, one transaction (the \emph{victim}) is aborted and retried;
          the others can then proceed. MySQL InnoDB and PostgreSQL use this approach.
    \item \textbf{Deadlock prevention}: enforce a global ordering on resources and require all
          transactions to acquire locks in that fixed order. If transaction A always locks Row 1
          before Row 2, and B does the same, no circular wait can form. Alternatively, use
          timestamps: the older (higher-priority) transaction waits; the younger one is aborted
          (``wound-wait'' or ``wait-die'' schemes).
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}
