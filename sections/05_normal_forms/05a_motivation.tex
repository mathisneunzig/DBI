\section{Normal Forms}

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
\begin{frame}{Why Normal Forms?}
  \begin{alertblock}{The Problem: Poorly Designed Relations}
    A na\"ively designed table that stores ``everything in one place'' leads to a
    range of systematic problems collectively called \textbf{anomalies}.
  \end{alertblock}

  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Data Redundancy:} The same fact (e.g.\ an artist's debut year)
          is stored in every row that mentions that artist.  This wastes storage
          and, more importantly, opens the door to inconsistency.
    \item \textbf{Update Anomalies:} If a fact is stored in $n$ places and we
          update only $k < n$ of them, the database becomes contradictory.
    \item \textbf{Insertion Anomalies:} We cannot record a new fact without also
          supplying unrelated data.  Example: we cannot add a new artist until
          that artist releases an album.
    \item \textbf{Deletion Anomalies:} Deleting a record accidentally removes
          other unrelated facts.  Example: deleting the last album of an artist
          also deletes all information about that artist.
    \item \textbf{Complex Queries:} Denormalised tables often require intricate
          SQL (self-joins, aggregations, deduplication) just to produce correct
          results for simple questions.
  \end{itemize}

  \begin{block}{A Problematic Table}
    \small
    \begin{tabular}{lllllll}
      \hline
      \textbf{ID} & \textbf{Title} & \textbf{Songs} & \textbf{Main Artist} & \textbf{Debut} & \textbf{Genre} & \textbf{Release} \\
      \hline
      A100 & Midnights (3am Ed.) & Would've\ldots; Karma & Taylor Swift & 2006 & Pop & 2022 \\
      A101 & 1989 (Taylor's Ver.) & Shake It Off; Blank Space & Taylor Swift & 2006 & Pop & 2023 \\
      \hline
    \end{tabular}
    \vspace{4pt}

    Two immediate violations: (1) multiple songs are packed into a single cell
    (\emph{atomicity violated}); (2) \texttt{Debut} repeats for every album of
    the same artist (\emph{redundancy}).
  \end{block}
\end{frame}

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
\begin{frame}{The Solution: Normal Forms}
  \begin{block}{What Are Normal Forms?}
    \textbf{Normal forms (NF)} are a series of progressive rules for organising
    relation schemas.  Each rule eliminates a specific class of redundancy or
    dependency problem.
  \end{block}

  \begin{columns}[t]
    \begin{column}{0.48\textwidth}
      \textbf{Goal}
      \begin{itemize}
        \item Reduce redundancy and unnecessary attribute dependencies
        \item Eliminate update, insertion, and deletion anomalies
        \item Produce a clean, maintainable schema
      \end{itemize}

      \vspace{6pt}
      \textbf{Key Insight}\\
      Normal forms are \emph{cumulative}: a relation in $n$NF must also satisfy
      all lower normal forms.  There is no skipping.
    \end{column}
    \begin{column}{0.48\textwidth}
      \textbf{Hierarchy of Normal Forms}
      \begin{itemize}
        \item Commonly used: 1NF, 2NF, 3NF, BCNF
        \item Less common: 4NF, 5NF
        \item Rarely used: 6NF, EKNF, ETNF, DKNF
      \end{itemize}

      \vspace{6pt}
      \textbf{Two Key Algorithms}
      \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Synthesis Algorithm} $\to$ 3NF\\
              (lossless \emph{and} dependency-preserving)
        \item \textbf{Decomposition Algorithm} $\to$ BCNF\\
              (lossless, but may not preserve all FDs)
      \end{itemize}
    \end{column}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}
